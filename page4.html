Countable & Uncountable Nouns (Food Focus)

What's the Difference?

In English, we classify nouns (like food items) as either countable or uncountable.

  • Countable nouns are things we can count individually (one apple, two apples, three apples...). They have singular and plural forms.
  • Uncountable nouns are things we cannot count individually (like liquids, powders, abstract concepts, or things considered as a whole mass like rice or bread). They usually only have a singular form.

Using Countable & Uncountable Nouns

  • With singular countable nouns, use a or an: an apple, a banana.
  • With plural countable nouns, use numbers, some, any, many, a few: three eggs, some olives, many grapes.
  • With uncountable nouns, use some, any, much, a little. They take a singular verb: Some cheese is on the table. Is there any rice? How much bread?
  • Never use a/an with uncountable nouns (a rice, a milk).
  • Some: Used in positive sentences (I have some bread.) and in offers/requests (Would you like some coffee?).
  • Any: Used in questions (Do you have any cheese?) and negative sentences (I don't have any juice.).
  • How much...? is used for uncountable nouns. (How much sugar?)
  • How many...? is used for plural countable nouns. (How many apples?)
  • To count uncountable nouns, use units: a piece of cheese, a bottle of water, a slice of bread, a kilo of rice.
