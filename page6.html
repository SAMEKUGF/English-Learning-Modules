Using CAN & CAN'T for Ability

What Can You Do?

We use the modal verb CAN to talk about ability – what someone knows how to do, or is able to do.

We use CAN'T (cannot) to talk about inability – what someone doesn't know how to do, or is unable to do.

Grammar Structure

Type Structure & Examples
Affirmative (+) Subject + can + base verb...
(Same form for all subjects: I, You, He, She, It, We, They)
  • I can play the violin.
  • She can sing very well.
  • They can cook delicious food.
Negative (-) Subject + can't (cannot) + base verb...
(Same form for all subjects)
  • He can't play golf.
  • We can't speak Chinese.
  • It (the computer) can't connect to the internet.
Yes/No Questions (?) Can + Subject + base verb...?
(Same form for all subjects)
  • Can you play golf? → Yes, I can. / No, I can't.
  • Can she do karate? → Yes, she can. / No, she can't.
Wh- Questions (?) Wh- word + can + Subject + base verb...?
(Asking about ability)
  • What can you do? → I can cook and draw.
  • Where can they play soccer? → They can play in the park.
Wh- word + can't + Subject + base verb...?
(Asking about inability - less common, but possible)
  • What can't he do? → He can't cook or draw.

Key Points to Remember

  • Use the base form of the verb after can and can't (e.g., play, not plays or playing).
  • Can and Can't are the same for all subjects (I, you, he, she, it, we, they). No "-s" for he/she/it!
  • Can't is the contraction of cannot.
